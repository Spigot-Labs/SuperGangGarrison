Compat stair-step detection correction (2026-04-19)
- Observation: even after allowing the waist predicate to consider stationary movement, `IsModernStairStepAhead(...)` always returns false when `horizontalSpeed == 0`.
- Source reference: clientBot_2020_update `botStep.gml` detects "stairs vs wall" by scanning forward from `player.object.x` until a collidable point, using `sign(player.object.hspeed)` as the scan direction.
  - In the original Step ordering, this can still be nonzero at the moment the AI decides to jump, even if collision resolution later zeroes it.
- Patch:
  - BotAI/BotController.ClientBot2020Compat.cs: add `CompatIsStairStepAhead(...)` which falls back to the requested `horizontal` direction when `horizontalSpeed == 0`, and use it for `compat:waist`, `compat:platform`, and the `s=` debug probe.

  Final Analysis: Why direct GML logic ports are regressing

  Despite achieving near-perfect algorithmic parity with the original GML (clientBot_2020_update) for weight expansion
  and jump selection, the test suite regressed significantly. I believe this is due to several fundamental differences
  between the GML environment and the C# port:

   1. Physics Timing & Order of Operations: In GML, sign(hspeed) is often checked in the same Step event before
      collision resolution zeroes it. In the C# port, the AI frequently observes a horizontalSpeed of exactly 0.0 when
      pressed against a wall. My "stair jump" and "stuck jump" fixes attempted to account for this, but the exact
      pixel-perfect timing of when a bot "hits" a wall vs. when it "jumps" is different in SimulationWorld.
   2. Heuristic Dependency on "Incorrect" BFS: The existing movement controller (TrySelectCompatNextPoint) contains
      numerous heuristics (like second_anchor_block, no_next_direct, and sticky points) that appear to have been "tuned"
      to the C# port's original (mathematically inverted) BFS behavior. By implementing a "correct" reverse-BFS, I
      steered the bots into paths that the movement controller wasn't tuned to handle, causing new oscillations.
   3. Graph Generation Nuances: The GML bot generates ReverseOnlyBlocked lists based on its own specific gap-detection
      logic. If the C# generator's gap detection differs by even a few pixels, a "correct" BFS expansion becomes a
      liability because it relies on those one-way edges being perfectly defined.
   4. Class Filtering Paradox: The GML original does not filter weight expansion by class, yet removing this filter in
      C# caused a massive regression. This suggests the C# nav mesh generation produces many "Scout-only" edges that,
      without filtering, lead Heavy and Pyro bots into inescapable local minima that they cannot jump out of.
   5. The "Final Mile" Problem: The GML bot's direct_target logic (weight <= 1) is extremely sensitive. My changes to
      next-point selection often penalized these transitions or tried to find "better" nav nodes, which ironically
      prevented the bots from actually touching the objective markers.

  Recommendation: Future efforts should move away from GML parity and instead focus on C#-native pathfinding (like the
